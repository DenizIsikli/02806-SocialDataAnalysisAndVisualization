## NARRATION SCRIPT FOR TEXT-TO-SPEECH

*new slide* Title: "What Bothers New Yorkers?"

Every day, thousands of New Yorkers file 311 complaints—reporting everything from broken heating to rodent infestations to broken streets. But what problems matter most? And does the city treat all neighborhoods fairly? This is a data-driven exploratory narrative—we're using actual data patterns to uncover stories about how cities work.

*new slide* Data Overview

For our analysis, we gathered 50,000 311 service complaints spanning five years—2020 to 2025—covering all five NYC boroughs and 514 community boards. The dataset contains 10 key variables with zero missing values, giving us excellent data quality to work with.

*new slide* Top Complaint Types Distribution

We analyzed 16 different complaint categories. Here's what stands out: heat, water, and rodent problems dominate—accounting for 13.5%, 11%, and 10.3% of all complaints respectively. Together, these three categories make up one-third of ALL complaints. The remaining complaints are distributed across 13 other types. This concentration tells us infrastructure is the real challenge facing the city.

*new slide* Geographic Distribution by Borough

Manhattan reports the most complaints at 25%, followed by Brooklyn at 22%, Queens and the Bronx at about 20% each, and Staten Island at 13%. But here's the key insight: more complaints doesn't mean more problems. Manhattan has the highest population density, which naturally leads to more reports. Geographic data reveals patterns, but equity matters more.

*new slide* Resolution Time Across Boroughs

This is our most important finding. NYC resolves complaints at nearly the same speed in every single borough—about 30 days on average across all five boroughs. The differences are negligible, less than one day. This is remarkable because it suggests fair resource allocation. Wealthy neighborhoods don't get faster service than low-income areas.

*new slide* Complaint Volume Over Time (2020-2025)

Looking at temporal distributions, complaint volume has remained stable over the entire five-year period. We see seasonal patterns—winter peaks in December through February with heating-related complaints, then summer lows in June through August. Fall and spring show moderate levels. This stability tells us the city is managing its urban challenges well—they're not getting worse, just predictable and seasonal.

*new slide* Why This Genre Works for Our Story

We chose a data-driven exploratory narrative because the story emerges directly from the data itself—not from opinions or illustrations, but from actual statistics about what residents complain about. We show you the data progressively, building from simple facts to complex insights. We don't just show numbers; we explain what they mean: that infrastructure matters, that equity exists, and that cities can improve by listening to their residents.

This data shows where to invest: upgrade heating systems, manage pests, fix water infrastructure. By understanding what bothers New Yorkers most, the city can build better.
