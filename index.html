When Does Crime Happen in San Francisco?

The City of San Francisco publishes detailed records of police incident reports, documenting when and where crimes occur, as well as how they are categorized. This dataset spans multiple years and includes information such as timestamps, locations, and crime types, allowing us to analyze patterns across both time and space.

In this short data story, we focus on a simple but important question: when does crime happen during the day? By examining how incidents are distributed across the 24 hours of a typical day, and how these patterns change (or remain stable) over time, we can uncover underlying structures in urban activity.

It is important to note that this dataset reflects reported incidents, not necessarily all crime that occurs. Changes in policing strategies, reporting behavior, or data collection practices can influence the patterns we observe. As a result, the findings presented here should be interpreted as patterns in recorded data rather than direct measures of underlying criminal activity.

Crime follows a daily rhythm

Crime in San Francisco is not evenly distributed throughout the day. Instead, it follows a clear and repeatable daily cycle that reflects how people move, work, and interact within the city. By aggregating incidents across multiple years and normalizing within each crime type, we focus on when crimes occur rather than how often they occur overall.

The figure below shows the average hourly distribution of different crime categories. Each line represents a specific crime type, normalized so that we focus on when crimes occur rather than how often they occur overall. This allows us to identify shared temporal structures across otherwise very different types of incidents.

Figure 1: Average hourly distribution of crime types. Across categories, crime is consistently lowest in the early morning (around 4–6 AM), increases steadily throughout the day, and peaks in the late afternoon and evening hours.

One of the most important patterns to notice is the sharp drop in activity during the early morning hours. Between roughly 4 AM and 6 AM, all crime types reach their lowest levels. This likely reflects reduced human activity - fewer people are outside, businesses are closed, and overall interaction is minimal. As the day begins, activity gradually increases, suggesting a close relationship between crime and the presence of people in public spaces.

From late morning into the afternoon, most crime categories show a steady rise. However, the most pronounced increase occurs in the late afternoon and evening period (approximately 4 PM to 8 PM), where multiple crime types reach their peak levels. This period corresponds to times when the city is most active: commuting, shopping, and social activities all contribute to increased opportunities for both interaction and conflict.

While the general pattern is shared, there are also subtle differences between crime types. For example, theft-related crimes tend to peak slightly earlier in the evening, aligning with commercial activity, whereas other categories may extend later into the night. These differences suggest that while overall crime follows human activity, specific categories are influenced by more localized behaviors and contexts.

Importantly, this figure shows relative timing, not absolute risk. Because the data is normalized, a higher value does not necessarily mean that a crime type is more frequent overall - only that it is more likely to occur at that specific time of day. This distinction is critical when interpreting patterns and avoids misleading conclusions about which crimes are “most common.”

Overall, the key takeaway is that crime follows a structured and predictable daily rhythm, with low activity in the early morning and peaks during the most active hours of the day. This pattern suggests that crime is closely tied to routine human behavior, rather than occurring randomly throughout time.

Where do these patterns happen?

Temporal patterns only tell part of the story. While we now understand when crime tends to occur, it is equally important to examine where these incidents are concentrated. Crime is not evenly distributed across San Francisco - instead, it forms clear spatial clusters that reflect the structure and activity of the city.

The heatmap below aggregates all incidents and highlights areas with higher concentrations of reported crime. Warmer colors (yellow to red) indicate higher density, while cooler colors (blue) represent lower activity. Rather than a uniform spread, we observe a highly uneven distribution, with distinct hotspots emerging across the city.

Figure 2: Spatial distribution of crime incidents in San Francisco. High-density clusters are concentrated in central and eastern parts of the city, while residential areas tend to show lower activity levels.

One key pattern to notice is the strong concentration of incidents in the central parts of the city, compared to the much more dispersed patterns in residential areas.

At a high level, the most prominent hotspot appears in the central and eastern parts of San Francisco, including areas such as downtown, SoMa, and the Mission district. These locations are characterized by high population density, commercial activity, and major transport routes - all factors that increase the likelihood of both interaction and reported incidents.

If we zoom in on the downtown and Tenderloin area, the density becomes particularly clear. The grid-like street structure shows continuous clusters of activity along major corridors, suggesting that crime is not isolated to single points but rather spreads along highly trafficked streets. This aligns with expectations: areas with nightlife, retail, and transit hubs naturally generate more opportunities for incidents.

Moving slightly west, neighborhoods such as Pacific Heights and the Western Addition show noticeably lower intensity. While incidents are still present, they are more dispersed and less concentrated. This contrast highlights how crime density is closely tied to land use - residential areas tend to have lower and more scattered activity compared to commercial centers.

If we instead examine the Richmond and Sunset districts in the western part of the city, the pattern becomes even more sparse. Here, the heatmap reveals isolated points rather than continuous clusters. These neighborhoods are primarily residential, with fewer commercial hubs and less dense foot traffic, which likely contributes to the lower concentration of incidents.

Looking toward the southern and southeastern areas, including parts of Bayview, we again observe localized clusters rather than widespread density. These appear around specific roads and intersections, suggesting that even in less dense areas, crime tends to concentrate around key infrastructural or commercial nodes rather than being evenly distributed.

Overall, the spatial distribution reinforces a key insight: crime is highly concentrated in areas with high human activity. However, it is important to interpret these patterns carefully. Higher density does not necessarily imply inherently “more dangerous” areas - it may also reflect increased reporting, policing presence, or simply the fact that more people are present to witness and report incidents.

Taken together, the map complements the temporal analysis by showing that crime is not only structured in time, but also strongly shaped by the physical and social geography of the city.

Patterns are stable over time

So far, we have seen that crime follows a clear daily rhythm. A natural next question is whether this pattern is stable, or if it changes significantly from year to year. To explore this, we look at how hourly crime distributions evolve over time using an interactive visualization.

When focusing on a subset of crime types, the same overall structure quickly becomes apparent. Most categories show a clear dip in the early morning hours, followed by a steady increase throughout the day and a peak in the late afternoon or evening. This mirrors the pattern observed in the static visualization, suggesting that the daily rhythm is not driven by a single category but is shared across many types of crime.

Even when isolating different groups of crimes, subtle differences emerge, but they occur within a consistent framework. Some categories rise more sharply in the afternoon, while others peak slightly later in the evening. However, these variations are relatively small compared to the overall structure, which remains remarkably stable.

When all crime types are viewed together, the consistency becomes even more striking. Despite differences in magnitude and small fluctuations, the curves align in a way that reinforces a shared temporal pattern. This indicates that the timing of crime is governed by broader, persistent factors, rather than short-term changes or isolated events.

Importantly, this visualization also highlights the distinction between changes in volume and changes in structure. While the number of incidents may increase or decrease over time, the underlying daily rhythm remains largely unchanged. This suggests that crime timing is closely tied to stable aspects of urban life, such as work schedules, commuting patterns, and social activity.

However, as with previous analyses, caution is needed when interpreting these results. Stability in recorded patterns does not necessarily imply stability in underlying behavior. Changes in policing strategies, reporting practices, or legal definitions can all influence the data without reflecting real changes in crime itself.

Overall, the key takeaway is that crime timing in San Francisco is highly stable over time. While yearly fluctuations exist, the fundamental daily structure persists, reinforcing the idea that crime is closely linked to consistent patterns of human activity.

What does this mean?

Taken together, the analyses reveal a clear and consistent picture: crime in San Francisco is neither random in time nor space. Instead, it follows structured patterns that align closely with human activity — increasing during the day, peaking in the evening, and concentrating in dense urban areas.

At first glance, one might interpret the evening peak as evidence that these hours are inherently more “dangerous.” However, this conclusion would be overly simplistic. The data reflects recorded incidents, which are influenced not only by actual behavior, but also by policing strategies, reporting practices, and the presence of people who can witness and report events.

For example, areas with higher police presence or more foot traffic may naturally generate more reports, even if underlying behavior is similar elsewhere. Likewise, changes in enforcement or policy over time can create apparent trends in the data that do not directly correspond to real changes in crime.

What we can say with greater confidence is that crime is strongly shaped by routine human activity. The stability of the daily rhythm across crime types and over time suggests that these patterns are not accidental, but instead reflect consistent aspects of how the city functions — work schedules, mobility, and social behavior.

This highlights an important takeaway for interpreting data more broadly: patterns in data are not always direct reflections of reality, but are often shaped by how that data is generated. Understanding both the strengths and limitations of the dataset is therefore essential when drawing conclusions.

Ultimately, the key insight is that crime follows structured and stable daily patterns. These patterns are useful for understanding urban dynamics, but they must be interpreted carefully and in context, rather than taken as direct measures of risk or causality.
